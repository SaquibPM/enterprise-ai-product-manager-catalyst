## PURPOSE

Great product leaders are great coaches. Your job isn't just shipping products; it's developing people who ship products, and developing people who develop people. Most PM managers struggle because they lack a clear competency framework (so performance feedback is subjective), don't conduct structured 1:1s (so coaching feels ad-hoc), and promote for tenure instead of competency (so they end up with burned-out veterans in senior roles). This skill gives you battle-tested frameworks for hiring, developing, and promoting PMs in enterprise settings—where domain expertise, stakeholder management, and systems thinking matter even more than in consumer products.

## KEY CONCEPTS

### 1. PM Competency Framework (The 6 Dimensions)

Every PM needs competency across six dimensions. The mix varies by level, but all matter:

**1. Discovery** (Finding problems worth solving)
- Junior: Can identify customer pain points through interviews; asks good clarifying questions
- Senior: Runs systematic discovery programs; synthesizes patterns across customer segments; generates strategic insights that shift company direction
- Staff/Principal: Creates discovery methodology used across organization; identifies markets or verticals others miss; thought leader on customer understanding

**2. Definition** (Turning problems into coherent product strategies)
- Junior: Writes clear feature requirements; structures tradeoffs clearly
- Senior: Writes vision documents and strategies; makes coherent prioritization calls; articulates "why this, why now, why us"
- Staff/Principal: Defines company-wide product strategy; translates business strategy into product roadmap; coaches other PMs on strategy

**3. Delivery** (Getting products shipped on time with quality)
- Junior: Communicates clearly with engineering; tracks progress; removes blockers
- Senior: Manages complex cross-functional launches; navigates trade-offs between speed and quality; ships with predictable cadence
- Staff/Principal: Defines delivery excellence across org; mentors teams through difficult launches; improves org velocity and quality

**4. Leadership & Influence** (Earning trust and moving organizations)
- Junior: Influences peer engineers and designers; shows up as reliable team member
- Senior: Influences executives, CFO, customers; navigates complex stakeholder dynamics; drives decisions without formal authority
- Staff/Principal: Shapes executive strategy; mentors leaders across org; represents function at board/investor level

**5. Technical Depth** (Understanding what's possible and what's hard)
- Junior: Understands technical concepts enough to ask smart questions; reads design docs; grasps system architecture
- Senior: Understands architectural trade-offs; can estimate engineering effort; identifies technical risks early
- Staff/Principal: Evaluates technology choices; mentors team on technical depth; identifies architectural decisions that matter for product

**6. Domain Expertise** (Knowing the industry, customer, and market)
- Junior: Learning the domain; asks "dumb" questions; benefits from mentoring on domain context
- Senior: Domain expert; knows customer workflows, compliance requirements, competitive landscape; customer trusts their advice
- Staff/Principal: Industry leader; visible in industry; pulls customers in based on domain credibility; writes the book (literally) on domain

**Enterprise-specific nuance**: Competencies 4 (Leadership), 5 (Technical Depth), and 6 (Domain Expertise) matter far more than in consumer. Enterprise sales take 6-12 months. Your PM must understand the customer's industry deeply enough to advise their CEO. Technical architecture decisions in enterprise have 5-year implications. You need strong leaders who can navigate 12-person buying committees.

### 2. PM Career Ladder

Most PM career ladders go: APM → PM → Senior PM → Staff PM → Principal PM → Director of Product

**But here's the problem**: Some of the best PMs never want to manage. And some of the best managers aren't the strongest individual contributors.

**Better approach**: Dual track (Individual Contributor vs. Manager)

```
Director of Product ──────┬──────── VP Product
                          │
Staff PM ─────────────────┼──────── Senior Product Manager (5-10 reports)
         │                │
      (IC track)          │ (Management track)
                          │
Senior PM ────────────────┼──────── Group Product Manager (3-5 reports)
         │                │
      (IC track)          │ (Management track)
                          │
PM ───────────────────────┼──────── Product Manager (1-2 reports)
         │                │
      (IC track)          │ (Management track)
                          │
APM (Associate PM) ───────┴──────── Associate Product Manager
```

**Why dual track matters**:
- Your best domain expert PM might not want to manage; they should be able to grow as Staff IC without managing people
- Your best coach might not want to ship products; they should be able to specialize in product operations or organization
- Management isn't a promotion for being good at your job; it's a different job that some people are better at

### 3. The 1:1 Framework (Structured Coaching)

Most PMs do 1:1s ad-hoc ("Hey, how's it going?" → 15 min of small talk → no real feedback).

**Better approach**: Structured 1:1 with clear agenda and consistency.

**1:1 Meeting Structure** (30 minutes, bi-weekly):

```
OPENING (5 min): Personal Check-In
- How are you doing? (Not "how's work?" but actual personal wellbeing)
- Anything I can support you with outside of work?
- This builds trust and shows you care about them as a person

BUSINESS & WINS (5 min): What's Working?
- What shipped this week? (Celebrate wins, even small ones)
- Any customer wins or feedback you're excited about?
- Any great collaboration or feedback from peers?

CHALLENGES & SUPPORT (10 min): What's Hard?
- What's blocking you or the team?
- Where do you need help or guidance?
- Any conflicts with cross-functional partners?
- Where do you feel underprepared?

DEVELOPMENT & COACHING (7 min): How Do We Grow You?
- What competency are we building right now?
- Feedback on recent work (specific, behavioral, actionable)
- Reading/resources/mentoring to advance development
- Career progression: How are you tracking toward next level?

CLOSING (3 min): Clarity & Next Steps
- Recap any asks or commitments you're making
- Calendar any follow-up conversations (1:1 with engineering lead, customer call, etc.)
```

**Bi-weekly vs. Weekly?**: Senior PMs do bi-weekly (they're self-directed). Junior/APMs do weekly (they need more frequent feedback and course-correction).

**Anti-patterns**:
- Manager does all the talking; never asks open-ended questions
- 1:1 becomes "status update" on projects (move to async slack or email)
- No structured feedback; coaching feels random
- Manager only talks about problems; never celebrates wins
- No development plan; team member doesn't know what they're working toward

### 4. Structured Performance Feedback (Quarterly or Annual)

Most PM feedback is vague ("You're doing well" / "You need to improve on communication"). That's not feedback; it's theater.

**Better approach**: Use competency framework + specific behavioral examples

**Feedback Format** (based on competency framework):

For each competency, rate 1-5:
- 1 = Below expectations for level
- 2 = Needs improvement
- 3 = Meets expectations
- 4 = Exceeds expectations
- 5 = Exceptional

Then provide:
1. **Specific example**: "In Q2, you ran 15 customer interviews on the procurement workflow. You synthesized findings into three clear segments..."
2. **Why it matters**: "This discovery work unblocked a $2M expansion opportunity with enterprise segment..."
3. **What to continue**: "Keep running structured discovery programs; you're building deep market insight"
4. **Where to grow**: "Your discovery excellence is clear. Next growth area: translating insights into written strategy docs. Let's work on that in Q3"

**Example Performance Review** (Senior PM):

| Competency | Rating | Evidence | Growth Area |
|------------|--------|----------|------------|
| Discovery | 4 | Ran 20 customer interviews; identified 3 new expansion opportunities; customer advisory board formed | Systematize findings into competitive intelligence |
| Definition | 4 | Wrote clear H1/H2 strategy; negotiated trade-offs with engineering effectively; OKRs well-written | Build skills in market sizing and TAM analysis |
| Delivery | 3 | Shipped Workday integration on-time; two minor overcommitments in roadmap | Improve estimation and stakeholder expectation-setting |
| Leadership | 4 | Influenced CFO on financial dashboards product; navigated complex customer requirements; peer feedback excellent | Develop executive presence; present to board |
| Technical Depth | 3 | Understands ERP integration complexity; identifies technical risks | Deeper technical learning; read more architecture docs |
| Domain Expertise | 5 | Procurement expert; customers ask for your advice; you speak at industry conferences | Leverage expertise to mentor junior PMs |

**Compensation & Promotion Decisions**: Use ratings to inform leveling decisions, not as the sole factor. A 4 or 5 on most competencies suggests readiness for next level. A 3 or below on leadership suggests not ready for manager role.

### 5. Hiring Rubric (Interviewing PMs)

When hiring PMs, you need to assess all six competencies, but signal them through past work examples.

**Interview Loop Structure** (4-5 interviews):

**1. Screening (30 min, Phone)**
- Walk me through a product you've shipped. Why did you choose to build it? What happened?
- Tell me about a time you had to say "no" to a customer or stakeholder. How did you navigate it?
- Why are you interested in this role / this company?
Purpose: Assess discovery + definition clarity + communication

**2. Discovery Deep Dive (60 min, Video)**
- Tell me about a time you identified a major market opportunity. How did you discover it? How did you validate it?
- Walk me through your discovery process. What tools/methods do you use? How do you synthesize findings?
Purpose: Assess discovery depth; ability to synthesize; curiosity

**Scoring Rubric for Discovery**:
- 4-5: Runs systematic discovery; identifies patterns others miss; translates to strategic insights
- 3: Runs adequate customer research; takes insights at face value; limited synthesis
- 2: Minimal customer research; relies on assumptions; weak evidence
- 1: No real discovery; builds based on intuition

**3. Execution Case Study (60 min, Video/In-person)**
- Present a product strategy you'd recommend for [domain]. Walk us through: (a) market opportunity, (b) customer validation, (c) your go-to-market strategy, (d) metrics for success, (e) risks
Purpose: Assess definition + leadership + domain learning ability

**Scoring Rubric for Definition**:
- 4-5: Clear market opportunity sized; strategy is coherent and defensible; articulates compelling "why"; identifies realistic risks
- 3: Reasonable strategy; decent clarity; could be more crisp on competitive positioning
- 2: Strategy is scattered; unclear customer focus; weak competitive analysis
- 1: No coherent strategy; confused on market opportunity

**4. Leadership & Influence (45 min, In-person)**
- Tell me about a time you had to influence a senior leader or customer without formal authority. What did you do? What was the outcome?
- Tell me about a difficult peer relationship. How did you navigate it?
Purpose: Assess stakeholder management; influence without authority; emotional intelligence

**Scoring Rubric for Leadership**:
- 4-5: Demonstrates high emotional intelligence; influences execs through data + credibility; navigates conflicts directly and professionally
- 3: Can influence peers; still learning how to influence up; avoids major conflicts
- 2: Struggles to influence; sometimes gets defensive; conflict avoidance
- 1: Cannot influence; low emotional intelligence; defensive/territorial

**5. Technical Depth Conversation (30-45 min, with Engineering Hiring Manager)**
- Walk us through a complex technical decision you've had to navigate. What was the trade-off? How did you decide?
- Tell me about a time you identified a technical risk early. How did you help the team address it?
Purpose: Assess technical fluency; ability to evaluate trade-offs

**Scoring Rubric for Technical**:
- 4-5: Understands architectural complexity; evaluates trade-offs thoughtfully; anticipates technical risks
- 3: Understands technical concepts; can follow engineer explanations; limited proactive architecture thinking
- 2: Struggles with technical depth; relies heavily on engineers for guidance
- 1: Not interested in technical; dismissive of engineering constraints

**Debrief & Leveling Decision**:

After all interviews, score each competency 1-5, then:
- If hiring APM (entry level): Expect 2-3 on most competencies; 3+ on discovery and definition
- If hiring PM (mid-level): Expect 3+ on all competencies; 4 on at least 2 (usually discovery or definition)
- If hiring Senior PM: Expect 3+ on all; 4+ on discovery, definition, leadership
- If hiring Staff PM: Expect 4+ on most; 5 on at least one (usually domain or leadership)

**Weighted scoring** for hiring:
- Discovery (25%): Most important; shows thinking clarity
- Definition (25%): Strategy articulation
- Leadership (20%): Can you move mountains without formal authority?
- Delivery (15%): Can you get it shipped?
- Technical + Domain (15%): Nice to have; can be learned

## APPLICATION

### Step 1: Evaluate Your Current Team Against Framework

Create a table for each PM on your team:

```
PM: [Name]       | Level: [APM/PM/Senior/Staff/Principal] | Tenure: [Months]

Competency       | Rating | Evidence | Gap
─────────────────┼────────┼──────────┼──────────────────
Discovery        |   3    | Regular customer calls; some systematic research | Needs deeper synthesis; weak competitor analysis
Definition       |   4    | Strong vision docs; clear strategy articulation |
Delivery         |   3    | Ships on-time; occasional scope creep |
Leadership       |   3    | Good with peers; still learning to influence execs |
Technical        |   2    | Learns as needed; relies on engineers |
Domain Expertise |   3    | Learning; asks good questions; not yet expert |
```

### Step 2: Identify Development Gaps

For each PM, identify:
1. **What they're strong at** (leverage and grow)
2. **What they need to develop** (for next level)
3. **One concrete focus for next 90 days**

Example:
- PM: Sarah Chen
- Strengths: Discovery (4), Definition (4), Domain (5)
- Gap: Leadership (2) - she's strong individually but struggles to influence executives
- 90-day focus: Present quarterly business review to board; lead partnership negotiation with Workday executive; 1:1 coaching on executive presence

### Step 3: Build Development Plans

For each PM, create a 90-day development plan:

```
PM: Sarah Chen    Level: Senior PM    Target: Principal PM in 12 months

GOAL: Develop executive leadership and influence skills to lead product strategy at board level

Specific Development Areas:
1. Executive Presence & Storytelling
   - Action: Present Q2 QBR to board (June 30)
   - Action: Present in industry conference panel (August)
   - Support: Weekly 1:1 coaching on presentation skills; work with external coach on speaking
   - Success: Board feedback score 4/5 on clarity and presence

2. Cross-Functional Leadership (ERP Partnership Management)
   - Action: Lead Workday partnership negotiations (Q2-Q3)
   - Action: Build and manage Workday executive relationship
   - Support: Monthly check-ins on partnership progress; 1:1 coaching on influence without authority
   - Success: Partnership agreement signed; co-selling SLA established

3. Strategic Thinking at Organization Level
   - Action: Lead 3-year platform strategy development
   - Action: Participate in board strategy discussions quarterly
   - Support: 1:1s focused on market analysis; competitive intelligence
   - Success: Platform strategy approved by board; influences Q3-Q4 resource allocation

Mentoring:
- Mentor: [Name of Principal PM or Chief Product Officer]
- Meeting: Monthly 1-hour sessions
- Topics: Executive communication, board presence, strategic thinking

Reading/Learning:
- "Good Strategy/Bad Strategy" (Rumelt)
- "Crucial Conversations" (Patterson et al.)
- Executive communication course (Toastmasters or Presentation Zen)

Milestones:
- Week 2: Identify external coach for presentation skills
- Week 4: QBR presentation to board; collect feedback
- Week 8: Workday partnership negotiation underway
- Week 12: 360 feedback on leadership; assess progress toward Principal level

```

### Step 4: Career Conversations (Quarterly)

Every quarter, have explicit career conversation with each PM:

**Quarterly Career Conversation Template** (30 minutes):

```
Q: "What's your career goal? Where do you want to be in 2-3 years?"
A: [Listen for clarity on Individual Contributor vs. Manager track; domain preferences; geography; etc.]

Q: "What's your assessment of your current level? What competencies are you strong in? Where do you need to grow?"
A: [Calibrate perception vs. your assessment; identify blind spots]

Q: "What's one thing you want to develop in the next 90 days?"
A: [Agree on specific, measurable development goal; connect to career progression]

Q: "What support do you need from me to achieve that goal?"
A: [Be specific on mentoring, resources, stretch opportunities you can provide]

Q: "How are you feeling about your current role? Anything we should discuss about engagement, compensation, workload?"
A: [Flag retention risks early]

Q: "Let's align on what next level looks like for you. Here are the competencies you need to develop..."
A: [Be honest about gaps; provide path to next level if ready; or explain what's holding them back]
```

## COMMON PITFALLS

**1. Promoting for Tenure, Not Competency**
- Problem: "John has been here 5 years; it's time for a promotion" (even though he's weak on leadership)
- Fix: Use competency framework. Next level requires 4+ on most competencies. If someone is weak on leadership but strong on delivery, promote them as Staff IC, not manager.

**2. No Structured 1:1s or Feedback**
- Problem: PM manager only gives feedback during annual review ("You need to work on communication")
- Fix: Bi-weekly 1:1s with structured agenda; quarterly career conversations; specific, behavioral feedback tied to competencies

**3. Vague Competency Assessments**
- Problem: "Jamie is great at discovery" (but no evidence or specifics)
- Fix: Rate on 1-5 scale; provide 2-3 specific examples of evidence; explain gaps clearly

**4. No Development Plans**
- Problem: Identifying gaps but not giving people tools to close them
- Fix: For each gap, create specific development plan (mentoring, coaching, stretch projects, learning resources, timeline, success metrics)

**5. Confusing Manager Track with IC Track**
- Problem: Your best technical PM feels forced to manage to advance
- Fix: Build dual tracks. Some of your strongest contributors should be Staff/Principal ICs managing no one.

**6. Hiring PMs Without Assessing All Competencies**
- Problem: Only assess discovery; ignore technical depth
- Fix: Interview loop assesses all 6 competencies; weight by role level and product context

**7. Ignoring Enterprise-Specific Competencies**
- Problem: Hiring a consumer PM and expecting them to excel in enterprise without domain expertise
- Fix: For enterprise, domain expertise is critical. Pair strong consumer PM with domain expert mentor; expect 6-month ramp-up.

**8. Coaching Only for Problems**
- Problem: 1:1s focus only on what's wrong or what needs to improve
- Fix: Celebrate wins, leverage strengths, build on what they're good at. Development comes from strengths-based growth, not only fixing weaknesses.

**9. No 360 Feedback**
- Problem: Manager assessment only; no peer/customer/engineer input
- Fix: Semi-annual 360 feedback from engineers, customers, peer PMs, and leadership. This provides balanced perspective.

**10. Career Conversations Only at Review Time**
- Problem: PM doesn't know what their next level looks like; surprised at annual review
- Fix: Quarterly career conversations; explicit discussion on leveling, compensation, development
