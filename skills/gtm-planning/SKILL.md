# Go-to-Market Planning for Enterprise B2B Launches

## Purpose

Enterprise B2B GTM planning is fundamentally different from consumer launches. You're orchestrating 6-12 month procurement cycles, navigating multi-stakeholder approvals, managing partner channel activation, and executing phased rollouts across customer segments with vastly different risk profiles. This skill teaches frameworks, checklists, and playbooks used by enterprise product leaders to launch complex capabilities into mature customer bases.

## Key Concepts

### Launch Tier Framework

**Tier 1: Major Platform Release (12-18 weeks)**
- Entirely new product line or major architectural shift
- $5M+ ARR addressable in year 1
- New go-to-market motion or channel strategy
- Examples: New AI module, platform acquisition, major redesign
- Investment: $200K-$500K+
- Success: 30%+ adoption within 180 days

**Tier 2: Significant Feature (8-12 weeks)**
- Major capability within existing product
- $1M-$5M ARR impact in year 1
- Existing customer base, minimal new personas
- Examples: Analytical capability, workflow optimization, key integration
- Investment: $75K-$200K
- Success: 15-20% adoption within 90 days

**Tier 3: Incremental Improvement (4-6 weeks)**
- Enhancement to existing workflows
- <$1M ARR impact
- No new personas, minimal training
- Examples: UI improvement, performance optimization, new report
- Investment: <$25K
- Success: Positive feedback, no escalations

### Enterprise-Specific Considerations

**Procurement Enablement**: Create Procurement Narrative addressing ROI methodology, implementation timeline, resource requirements, vendor risk, SLA terms. Build Economic Impact Models: "For $500M procurement department, AI invoice matching drives 2% cost reduction ($10M annual savings) within 12 months."

**Partner Channel Activation**: Create Partner Positioning Kit with positioning, customer pitch, objection handlers. Define co-selling process. Establish revenue terms early—ambiguity kills partner enthusiasm.

**Compliance Certification Timing**: Map certifications (SOC 2, HIPAA, SOX, FedRAMP) before launch. SOC 2 audit requires 3-4 months for new controls. Plan launches around certification windows or communicate "available with certification expected by [date]."

**Phased Rollout by Segment**: Never big-bang. Segment: Strategic (top 20, direct enablement), Core (next 80, standard), Long-tail (SMB, self-service).

## Application

### Launch Tier Decision Framework

1. Creates new customer acquisition motion or channel? → Tier 1
2. Unlocks >$1M expansion revenue from existing base? → Tier 2
3. Requires new sales personas or compliance certification? → Tier 2
4. <$1M revenue and minimal training? → Tier 3

### GTM Timeline (Tier 1 Example)

Work backward from launch:
- Week 18: Launch, sales kickoff, customer communications
- Week 17: Final QA on materials
- Week 16: Beta program wraps
- Week 15: Sales enablement launch
- Week 14: Release notes finalized
- Week 13: Migrate from feature flags
- Week 12: Positioning locked, ROI validated
- Week 11: Marketing content begins
- Week 10: Product spec finalized
- Weeks 8-9: Marketing/sales briefs, customer advisory
- Weeks 1-7: Development, market research

## Worked Example: AI-Powered Spend Analytics Launch (Tier 1)

**Context**: Enterprise procurement platform launching AI spend analytics. Data science built anomaly detection predicting 3-5% category savings. $8M addressable within existing customers year 1.

### Launch Tier Justification

✓ New analytical capability
✓ $8M Y1 expansion revenue
✓ New training for multiple personas
✓ Data security review required
✓ Compliance review required
✓ **TIER 1 (18-week cycle)**

### Phase 1: Market Readiness (Weeks 1-4)

**Positioning Draft**
- Core: "Turn procurement spend data into AI-driven competitive advantage. Identify $10M+ in hidden savings."
- Economics: "For $500M procurement function, Spend Analytics drives 3-4% category savings. $500M × 3.5% = $17.5M annual. Implementation: 4 weeks. ROI: <2 months."
- Key Buyer: VP Procurement, Chief Procurement Officer
- Secondary: Category Manager (practitioner), CFO (economic)

**Target Segments**
1. Strategic Enterprise ($50M+ spend): Highest maturity, adoption-willing, significant savings
2. Mid-Market ($5M-$50M): Price-sensitive, ROI-focused, 6-month cycle
3. Verticals: Manufacturing (vendor complexity), Healthcare (supply chain), Retail (scale)

**Competitive Positioning**
- Incumbent: Coupa (good reporting, weak anomaly detection)
- Competitor: Jaggr (purpose-built, expensive, limited integration)
- Advantage: AI on your data, integrated workflows, actionable

**Compliance/Security**
- Customer data stays in tenant (no cross-customer training)
- ML model audit trail (GDPR Article 22)
- Data lineage documentation (SOC 2)
- Vendor data encryption in transit/at rest

### Phase 2: Internal Enablement (Weeks 5-10)

**Sales Enablement Battlecard**

SITUATION: Spend optimization opportunities

POSITIONING: "AI Spend Analytics automatically identifies 3-4% hidden category savings through anomaly detection and opportunity scoring."

PROOF POINTS:
- Manufacturing: $12M identified in 90 days
- Healthcare: Vendor onboarding 6 weeks → 2 weeks
- Detected $2.3M duplicate invoice in week 1

VALUE QUANTIFICATION:
- $50M annual spend → 3.2% = $1.6M potential
- Implementation: $150K
- Effort: $75K
- Year 1 ROI: 566% ($1.5M / $225K)
- Payback: <2 months

OBJECTION HANDLING:

"We already have spend analytics"
→ "Analytics shows what happened. Spend Analytics shows what's unusual. Like financial audit vs. spreadsheet."

"Worried model makes mistakes"
→ "Model scores 0-100. Your team reviews high-scoring items. It's prioritization, not decisions. One customer reviews 2% of transactions, catches errors, reduces work 85%."

"What about AI bias?"
→ "We audit monthly, require customer approval of rules. Model scores on performance metrics you configure."

COMPETITIVE DIFFERENTIATION:

vs. Coupa: Win on AI actionability, vendor risk scoring. Position as add-on module.

vs. Jaggr: Win on workflow integration. 30-40% lower cost (included in platform).

PRICING:
- Base: $200K-$400K annual (5 categories)
- Expansion: $50K per category
- Discount: 15% multi-year, 10% quick decision
- Structure: 3-year recommended

**Demo Script Framework**

DISCOVERY (15 min):
1. Category Discovery: "Walk through top 5 categories. Most focused on optimizing?"
2. Pain: "In [category], how many suppliers? Determine fair pricing?"
3. Buyer: "Who makes category strategy decisions?"
4. Timeline: "If we showed $500K savings next quarter, timeline?"

PAIN VALIDATION (10 min):
"I hear: (1) lots of data but hard to find patterns, (2) want to optimize but manual takes weeks. Right? What if you found opportunities 10x faster?"

SOLUTION DEMO (15 min):
1. Data Upload: "30 days invoice/PO data. Secure file upload."
2. Dashboard: "Spend by vendor, category, business unit. Surprises?"
3. AI Model: "Every line item scored 0-100. How normal is this given patterns?"
4. Anomaly: "$15K from Vendor A vs. $8K Vendor B last month. Model scored 87/100. Manual would take 100s hours."
5. Opportunity Scoring: "12 vendors same service, 40% price spread. Model flags consolidation. One customer consolidated 3 vendors, saved $2.1M."
6. Explainability: "Why 73/100? Price 35% above peer, invoice 2x normal, vendor new. Team can argue. 'Rush premium'—OK, model learns."

VALUE QUANTIFICATION (10 min):
"$50M annual. Conservative: 3% waste = $1.5M. We find 50-80% = $750K-$1.2M. Implementation $225K. Timeline: weeks 1-2 setup, 3-4 first recs, 5+ quick wins."

NEXT STEPS:
"Most valuable: POC in [category], or CFO call on economics?"

**CS Playbook for Adoption**

ONBOARDING (Weeks 1-4):

Week 1: Setup
- Customer provides 90-day export
- CS loads, validates completeness
- Data governance: Which categories first? Access?

Week 2: Model Training
- Model trains on customer's spend
- CS reviews top 20 anomalies
- Adjust sensitivity if needed

Week 3-4: Recommendations
- Dashboard live
- Category manager reviews top 30
- CS schedules 3 coaching calls: interpretation, action, success metrics

30-DAY CHECK-IN:
- Reviewed? Acted on?
- Estimated vs. realized savings
- Working well? Adjustments?

SUCCESS METRICS:

Category Manager:
- Anomalies reviewed/week: 20+
- Opportunities acted on/month: 3+
- Manual analysis time: trending down

Procurement Director:
- Total savings identified
- ROI realization
- Adoption across categories

CFO:
- Savings impact on P&L
- Spend visibility
- Procurement efficiency

## Common Pitfalls

### Anti-Pattern 1: Launching Without Sales Enablement

**What Happens**: Sales doesn't know how to sell. Continue old features. New capability unused.

**Why**: Sales teams sell what they're trained on.

**Fix**: 
- Sales kickoff 1 week before
- Battlecard 2 weeks before
- ROI calculator tested with 5-10 reps
- Track CRM usage; sales leader owns fixing

### Anti-Pattern 2: Big-Bang Rollouts

**What Happens**: Feature flipped for everyone. Some love, some confused. Support flooded. Rollback.

**Why**: Enterprise customers risk-averse.

**Fix**:
- Phase 1: Strategic only (top 10, direct support)
- Phase 2: Core (next 50, standard)
- Phase 3: Long-tail (self-service)
- Monitor before advancing

### Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring Procurement Cycles

**What Happens**: July launch. Customers interested. Budget review September. No budget. 6-month delay.

**Why**: Enterprise procurement scheduled.

**Fix**:
- Identify fiscal year calendars
- If off-budget: communicate now, activate after planning
- Prioritize budget-mode customers for early access

### Anti-Pattern 4: CS Team Not Trained Day 1

**What Happens**: Customers activate. Have questions. CS confused. Escalations. Confidence drops.

**Why**: CS is front line.

**Fix**:
- CS training 2 weeks before
- Create playbook, test with early customers
- Dedicated CS point person
- Product available for escalations

### Anti-Pattern 5: No Rollback Plan

**What Happens**: Critical bug. No rollback. 4 hours broken.

**Why**: Complex features have edge cases.

**Fix**:
- Identify steps 1 week before
- Test in staging
- Define go/no-go criteria
- Designate rollback owner
