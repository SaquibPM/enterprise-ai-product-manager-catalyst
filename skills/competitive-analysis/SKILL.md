## Purpose & Context

Most PMs do competitive analysis wrong. They build feature comparison matrices (Competitor A has 23 features, we have 24) and miss what actually matters in enterprise: *Why did they win that $2M deal and we didn't? What will make procurement green-light us over the incumbent?*

Enterprise competitive analysis requires understanding:
- **Deal dynamics**: Where do you win? Where do you lose? Why?
- **Buyer committee alignment**: Does a competitor appeal to the CFO but alienate IT? You need to know.
- **Procurement gating**: Which competitors have "approved vendor" status? Which are blocked by legal/security requirements?
- **Ecosystem lock-in**: Are you competing against a best-of-breed point solution or a platform play that's already integrated with their ERP?
- **Hidden moves**: What are competitors shipping that won't show up in your demo until they're in your deals?

This skill teaches you to build competitive profiles that answer the questions that actually matter for product, GTM, and positioning decisions.

## Competitive Scoring Matrix

Use this to score multiple competitors on the dimensions that actually matter to enterprise buyers.

### Step 1: Identify Scoring Dimensions

Not all features matter equally. Enterprise buyers care about:

**Functional Dimensions (What it does):**
- Invoice matching accuracy / automation rate
- Exception handling intelligence
- Multi-entity consolidation
- Vendor master data management
- Real-time analytics & reporting
- 3-way match configuration flexibility
- Integration breadth (# of ERP/accounting systems)
- Mobile/remote approval capabilities

**Non-Functional Dimensions (How it works):**
- Implementation time to value
- Data security & compliance certifications
- Uptime SLA guarantee
- API maturity & documentation
- Scalability (max volume)
- Multi-tenant vs. single-tenant isolation
- Data residency options
- Support availability & SLAs

**Commercial Dimensions (What it costs):**
- Total cost of ownership (3-year)
- Pricing transparency (clear vs. "call us")
- Payment flexibility
- ROI payback period
- Implementation cost
- Hidden costs (professional services, data migration)

**Strategic Dimensions (Where they fit):**
- Ecosystem integration (fits with existing stack)
- Vendor stability & financial health
- Roadmap alignment with your direction
- Procurement/compliance pre-approval status
- Change management support

### Step 2: Create the Matrix

Rate each competitor 1-5 on each dimension:
- 1 = Significantly behind
- 2 = Behind
- 3 = On par
- 4 = Ahead
- 5 = Significantly ahead

| Dimension | Weight | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C | Us |
|-----------|--------|---|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | | | | | |
| Invoice matching accuracy | 10% | 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 |
| Exception handling | 10% | 5 | 3 | 4 | 4 |
| Vendor master data mgmt | 8% | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| Real-time analytics | 8% | 4 | 3 | 2 | 3 |
| Integration breadth | 10% | 3 | 5 | 2 | 3 |
| **Non-Functional** | | | | | |
| Implementation speed | 8% | 2 | 4 | 3 | 4 |
| Security/compliance | 10% | 5 | 4 | 3 | 4 |
| API maturity | 8% | 4 | 3 | 2 | 3 |
| **Commercial** | | | | | |
| Pricing value (total cost) | 10% | 4 | 3 | 5 | 3 |
| **Strategic** | | | | | |
| Ecosystem fit | 8% | 4 | 4 | 3 | 3 |
| **TOTAL WEIGHTED SCORE** | 100% | **3.7** | **3.6** | **3.2** | **3.6** |

**How to Read This:**
- Competitor A and Us are neck-and-neck (3.7 vs. 3.6)
- Competitor A beats us in security, exception handling, ecosystem fit
- We beat Competitor A in vendor master data management
- Competitor C is weak across the board
- Competitor B beats on integration breadth; weak on exception handling

### Step 3: Identify Your Differentiation

From the matrix, calculate:

**Dimensions where we score 4-5 and competitors are 2-3:**
- Vendor master data management (us 5, all others 2-3)
- Exception handling (us 4, Comp B is 3)
- *Battlecard*: "For companies managing 500+ vendors with complex contract terms, our master data module cuts integration time by 40% and reduces payment exceptions by 20%"

**Dimensions where all competitors are weak (2-3):**
- Real-time analytics (us 3, all others 2-3)
- *Opportunity*: This is table-stakes; we should improve here

**Dimensions where a competitor significantly leads:**
- Competitor B on integration breadth
- *Challenge*: We need to either match this or convince buyers it's not essential

## RFP Win/Loss Analysis

In enterprise, RFP results reveal where you're winning and where you're vulnerable. Analyze patterns.

### Data Collection Framework

Track every deal where you responded to an RFP or competed against known competitors:

| Deal ID | Deal Name | RFP Issued | Won By | ACV | Customer Size | Vertical | Your Score | Comp A Score | Comp B Score | Key Decision Factor | Lost Because (if applicable) |
|---------|-----------|-----------|--------|-----|---|------|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2025-001 | Acme MidCorp AP | Q4 2024 | Competitor A | $500K | Mid-market | Manufacturing | 87/100 | 91/100 | 78/100 | Implementation speed | Vendor already approved; lower risk perception |
| 2025-002 | Baker Corp Procurement | Q4 2024 | Us | $1.2M | Enterprise | Financial Services | 94/100 | 85/100 | 89/100 | Security posture + ecosystem | Beat on SOC2 timeline + NetSuite integration |
| 2025-003 | Carter Industries | Q1 2025 | Competitor B | $300K | Mid-market | Retail | 82/100 | 88/100 | 91/100 | Price + implementation | They were 40% cheaper; faster time to first invoice |

### Win/Loss Pattern Analysis

**Question 1: Where are we winning?**
- Vertical(s): [Which industries favor us?]
- Company size(s): [Enterprise? Mid-market? Both?]
- Competitive situation(s): [Which competitors do we beat consistently?]
- Features that matter: [What RFP requirements favor us?]
- Deal characteristics: [Longer sales cycle, requires deep customization—plays to our strength?]

**Example Insight:**
- We're winning 75% of RFPs in Financial Services (vs. 40% in Manufacturing)
- We're winning vs. Competitor B (65% win rate) but losing to Competitor A (35% win rate)
- When security/compliance is a top-5 RFP requirement, we win 80%; when it's not mentioned, we win 45%
- *Action*: Shift GTM to emphasize security; focus on FS vertical; develop anti-Competitor-A positioning

**Question 2: Where are we losing?**
- Vertical(s): [Manufacturing? Retail?]
- Company size(s): [Are we losing to smaller startups because we're too "enterprise"?]
- Competitive situation(s): [Who's beating us? In what scenarios?]
- RFP requirements that hurt us: [Mobile app? Specific ERP integration?]
- Deal characteristics: [Faster implementation required? Price-sensitive?]

**Example Insight:**
- We're losing in Retail (35% win rate) because most RFPs require Oracle integration (we're weak here)
- When price is a top-5 RFP requirement, we lose 60% to Competitor B (who's 30% cheaper)
- Our implementation time (120 days) is too long for mid-market customers who need 45-day deployments
- *Action*: Either invest in Oracle connector or de-emphasize Retail; revise pricing strategy; create "fast track" implementation

### Win/Loss Interview Template

After significant wins/losses, interview the customer who decided.

**Questions for Wins:**
1. "Walk me through your evaluation process. What were the top criteria?"
2. "How did you evaluate us vs. [Competitor A] and [Competitor B]?"
3. "What was the deciding factor?"
4. "Were there things about [Competitor X] that almost swayed you?"
5. "What would have made the decision go the other way?"

**Questions for Losses:**
1. "I'd like to understand what led you to choose [Competitor X]. No hard feelings—just learning."
2. "What were the top criteria in your evaluation?"
3. "How did you evaluate us vs. them?"
4. "Was there a specific moment when you decided to go with them?"
5. "What would have needed to change for us to win?"
6. "If you were to upgrade or replace [Competitor X] in 12 months, would you evaluate us again?"

## Battlecard Template

Use this in sales conversations when facing a specific competitor. Keep it to 1 page.

### Battlecard: Us vs. Competitor A

**Situation:** [E.g., "Finance leader considering Competitor A due to lower price and faster implementation"]

**Our Positioning:**
> "We beat Competitor A on long-term ROI because our exception handling reduces manual work by 40%, not 20%. Over 3 years, that's $500K in labor savings vs. their $200K. Yes, they're cheaper upfront, but you'll spend more on staff."

**Strengths We Can Lean On:**
- Exception handling accuracy: Our AI understands context (fuel surcharges, multi-shipment consolidation) vs. their rule-based approach
- Vendor master data: We unify vendor info across all your entities; they require manual sync
- Customer success: 92% stay with us; they have 78% renewal rate
- Implementation: 90 days to first invoice; they're 60 days but with 40% longer TCO ramp-up

**Competitor A's Likely Positioning Against Us:**
- "Faster to value" (60 days to first invoice vs. our 90)
- "Lower price" ($300K ACV vs. our $500K)
- "Easier integration" (claims simpler SAP connector setup)

**How We Counter:**
- **On speed:** "Their speed is a mirage. 60 days to their first invoice, but 180 days to full AP automation. We're 90 days to full value."
- **On price:** "They're cheaper because they're less sophisticated. You'll pay $200K in consulting fees to make their rules engine work."
- **On integration:** "Let's do a side-by-side integration POC with your SAP team. We're confident we finish in <30 days."

**Questions to Ask to Disqualify Them:**
1. "How many vendors have you configured in Competitor A's system?" (If <100, they're early in ramp-up)
2. "What's your current exception rate with them?" (If >15%, ask why)
3. "Have you calculated your total cost of ownership including staff time?" (Most haven't)

**Our Close:**
- Offer a POC with their real invoices
- Run their exception handling through both systems side-by-side
- Calculate true TCO (ours will look better)
- Get their CFO aligned on 3-year ROI vs. Year 1 price

## Market Share Dynamics & Competitive Positioning

### TAM Shift Analysis

If Competitor A is displacing us in [segment], calculate the impact:

- Current market size (SAM) for [segment]: $X billion
- Competitor A's market share: [Y%]
- Your current market share: [Z%]
- Competitor A's growth rate vs. yours (last 12 months): [Comp A +40% YoY, You +15% YoY]

**Question:** Are they:
- Growing faster because they're eating *our* lunch?
- Growing faster because they're expanding the market?
- Growing faster because they're entering new verticals/geographies you don't serve?

**Analysis:**
- If they're taking share from us in existing segments, this is urgent
- If they're growing in new segments, we can co-exist
- If they're market-expanding, we should consider partnership

### Competitive Threats by Segment

Create a heat map:

| Segment | Your Share | Comp A Share | Comp B Share | Threat Level | Action |
|---------|---|---|---|---|---|
| Financial Services, Enterprise | 45% | 30% | 15% | Low | Maintain leadership; invest in moat |
| Manufacturing, Mid-Market | 25% | 50% | 15% | High | Urgent: Fix product gaps or retarget |
| Retail, SMB | 10% | 35% | 40% | De-prioritize | Extract value; don't invest in share gain |
| Public Sector | 5% | 15% | 20% | Low | Not a core market for any of us |

## Anti-Patterns in Competitive Analysis

### Anti-Pattern 1: Feature Comparison = Competitive Strategy

**What it looks like:**
- Competitor A has 23 features; you count and make a list of 25 features
- You claim "we have more than they do"
- Sales reps say "we beat them on feature X, Y, and Z"

**Why it's dangerous:**
- Enterprise buyers don't buy features; they buy outcomes
- A competitor with 12 well-built features beats you with 50 half-baked ones
- You miss the real story (e.g., they beat you because they're already pre-approved by procurement)
- Your roadmap becomes reactive: chasing feature parity instead of building moats

**How to fix it:**
- Stop doing feature scorecards
- Instead, ask: "In deals we lost to them, what was the actual blocker?"
- Answer: "Almost never a missing feature. Usually: lower price, faster deployment, existing relationship, procurement pre-approval"
- Build moats in outcomes, not features

### Anti-Pattern 2: Ignoring Ecosystem & Platform Dynamics

**What it looks like:**
- You analyze a point solution competitor in isolation
- You miss that they're acquired by or integrated with a larger platform player
- You don't realize your customer's ERP vendor is pushing their "native" solution
- By the time you notice, the competitor has 40% of your sales pipeline

**Why it's dangerous:**
- In enterprise, ecosystem fit matters more than feature excellence
- A "best of breed" solution loses to a mediocre "native" integration
- You're competing on features; they're competing on total cost of ownership and integration
- Your GTM strategy focuses on product when you should focus on partnerships

**How to fix it:**
- Map the ecosystem: Who owns the customer's ERP? What integrations matter?
- Understand the "gravity wells" in your market (SAP, Oracle, NetSuite, Salesforce pull customers toward their ecosystems)
- Build or partner to address ecosystem gaps
- Pivot messaging: "We integrate deeply with your existing stack" > "We have more features"

### Anti-Pattern 3: Analyzing Competitors You Don't Actually Compete Against

**What it looks like:**
- You build detailed profiles of 10 competitors
- You find out in Q4 that you only actually face 3 of them in real deals
- You've spent 400 hours analyzing companies your customers aren't even evaluating
- You haven't built a profile of the one competitor you lost 60% of deals to

**Why it's dangerous:**
- Wasted analysis effort
- You're not building the battlecards that matter
- Sales team gets confused by analysis of non-relevant competitors
- You miss emerging competitors that actually threaten you

**How to fix it:**
- Start with win/loss data: Which competitors show up in real RFPs?
- Only build detailed profiles for competitors in your top 5 by deal frequency
- Tier your competition: Tier 1 (weekly), Tier 2 (monthly), Tier 3 (quarterly)
- Update your competitive landscape as your ICP and GTM evolve

### Anti-Pattern 4: Confusing "Perceived Weakness" with "Real Weakness"

**What it looks like:**
- Market perception: "Competitor A's implementation is slow"
- You put it in a battlecard: "They take 120 days; we take 90 days"
- Your sales team repeats it in every deal
- Customer signs with them anyway because their implementation was actually 75 days (your data was wrong)

**Why it's dangerous:**
- You lose credibility when your competitive claims fall apart
- Customers believe the competitor, not you
- You miss the real reason you lost (maybe it was price, not speed)
- You build false confidence in sales team

**How to fix it:**
- Source competitive claims from customer interviews, not rumors
- If you say "they're slow," have evidence: "3 out of 4 customers told us their implementation took 150+ days"
- Distinguish: "Market perception is X; our customers report Y"
- Test claims in pilots and POCs

## Competitive Intelligence Sources

### Tier 1: High-Confidence Data
- Win/loss interviews with your customers
- Customer references from the competitor's website
- Product demos and trials (direct experience)
- Analyst reports (Gartner Magic Quadrant, etc.)
- Public financial disclosures (if they're public)

### Tier 2: Medium-Confidence Data
- Competitor job postings (hiring signals)
- G2/Capterra reviews and ratings
- Industry analyst reports (Forrester Wave, etc.)
- Your sales team's deal notes
- Competitor press releases and earnings calls

### Tier 3: Low-Confidence Data
- Rumor/hearsay from industry connections
- Assumptions based on their marketing messaging
- Feature counts and comparisons without customer validation
- Pricing intelligence from salespeople ("They quoted $X")

**Rule:** Only build battlecards on Tier 1 and Tier 2 data. Don't spread claims from Tier 3.

## Competitive Monitoring Cadence

**Weekly:** Track competitive deal activity
- How many RFPs include [Competitor A]?
- Did we win or lose? Why?
- What's the latest complaint from a customer about them?

**Monthly:** Update competitive profiles
- New product releases?
- Personnel changes?
- PR/analyst mentions?
- Win/loss pattern changes?

**Quarterly:** Full competitive review
- Refresh competitive scoring matrix
- Analyze win/loss trends
- Update battlecards
- Share findings with exec team

**Annually:** Deep dive
- Update all competitive profiles
- Recalculate TAM implications
- Reassess competitive threats
- Plan competitive response (product, pricing, positioning)
