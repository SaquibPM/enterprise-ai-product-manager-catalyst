## Purpose & Context

Enterprise product discovery is fundamentally different from consumer or SMB discovery. You're not just interviewing the user who'll touch your product daily—you're navigating a buying committee that might include procurement, IT security, the CFO's office, and line-of-business leaders. Their interests conflict. Their risk profiles differ. Your job is to uncover not just what they *want* to buy, but what *problem* they're actually trying to solve and why existing solutions fall short.

This skill teaches you to:
- **Design interviews that reach both buyers and users** without conflating their needs
- **Identify Jobs-to-be-Done** using a structured approach that surfaces emotional and social dimensions, not just functional features
- **Navigate enterprise realities** like multi-step procurement cycles, vendor lock-in concerns, and integration complexity
- **Avoid the champion trap** where you only hear from the enthusiast who wants your product—and miss the skeptics who'll block the deal
- **Extract actionable insights** from interview transcripts without falling into confirmation bias

## Core Framework: Teresa Torres Continuous Discovery Habits Interview Structure

### Interview Phases & Question Templates

**Phase 1: Opening & Context (2-3 minutes)**

Goal: Build rapport, establish psychological safety, explain confidentiality, set expectations about your role (you're learning, not selling).

Sample opening (adapt to context):
> "Thanks for taking the time to talk. I'm learning about how [invoice matching / expense management / vendor analytics] works in your organization. There's no right answer to my questions—I'm just trying to understand your current reality. This conversation is confidential; I won't share what you say with your competitor, and nothing you tell me will affect your current contract. Make sense?"

Key opening questions:
1. "Walk me through a typical week in your role. What does success look like for you?"
2. "How long have you been in this role? What's changed in your function over the past 18 months?"
3. "If I were sitting here 12 months from now and you told me this year was a huge success for your team, what would have had to happen?"

**Phase 2: Exploration & Context Dig (8-12 minutes)**

Goal: Understand the landscape before discussing your solution. Uncover current workflows, workarounds, pain points, and job context.

Exploration questions:
4. "Walk me through the entire [invoice matching] process from the moment an invoice lands to when it's paid. Don't skip steps—I want to know every system it touches."
5. "Where do things break down in that process today? Which step causes the most headaches?"
6. "Tell me about a recent invoice that took a really long time to match. Why did it take so long?"
7. "How do you measure whether this process is working well or poorly? What's your main metric?"
8. "Who else is involved in this process? Who cares if this works or doesn't?" (Map the stakeholder network)

For **buyers specifically** (procurement, IT, finance):
9. "What were the top three requirements your team had when evaluating [vendor category] solutions? How did you prioritize them?"
10. "Walk me through your procurement and integration process. From RFP to go-live—how long does that typically take?"
11. "What have been your biggest challenges with [incumbent vendor]? Why haven't you switched?"
12. "How do you evaluate security, compliance, and integration risk when considering a new vendor?"

For **users specifically** (AP team, expense auditors, account payable specialists):
13. "In your day-to-day work, what's your biggest frustration?"
14. "Tell me about a time you had to do something manually because the system couldn't handle it."
15. "If I gave you a magic wand and could automate one thing in your job, what would it be and why?"

**Phase 3: Validation & Hypothesis Testing (5-8 minutes)**

Goal: Test your emerging hypotheses without leading. Introduce your solution carefully.

Validation approach (Torres calls this "critical incident" method):
16. "I'm hearing that invoice exceptions—invoices that don't match PO or receipt—cause a lot of manual work. Is that fair? How often does that happen?"
17. "If we could reduce the time spent on exception handling by 60%, how would that change your month?"
18. "What would need to be true for you to consider changing your current approach? What's the bar?"

**Important**: Only introduce your product after understanding their world. When you do:
19. "We're building something that [value prop]. Based on what you've described, would this matter to you? Why or why not?"
20. "What would it take for you to believe this actually works? What would you need to see?"

**Phase 4: Closing & Commitment (2-3 minutes)**

Goal: Leave the door open. Identify next steps. Build relationship.

21. "Is there anything else I should be asking to understand your situation better?"
22. "If I had a prototype in a few weeks, would you be open to looking at it? No pressure."
23. "Can I reach out in a month or two if I have follow-up questions?"

## Jobs-to-be-Done Framework for Enterprise Interviews

JTBD isn't just "they need to match invoices." It's deeper:

### The Three Job Categories

**Functional Job** (What are they trying to accomplish?)
- "Match 99%+ of invoices automatically without PO or receipt"
- "Reduce the time our team spends on invoice exceptions from 6 hours/day to 2 hours/day"
- "Integrate with SAP without a $500K implementation project"

**Emotional Job** (How do they want to feel?)
- "Confident that we're not paying duplicate invoices"
- "Respected by the CFO for running a tight ship"
- "Less stressed about month-end close because invoices are handled"

**Social Job** (What do they want others to think of them/their team?)
- "Perceived as modern and efficient, not stuck with legacy processes"
- "Known for bringing AI solutions that actually work (not vaporware)"
- "Someone who improves cash flow, not someone asking for more headcount"

### How to Extract JTBD During Interviews

Listen for these signals:

**Functional signals:**
- Metrics they measure ("We're at 87% automation today")
- Workflows they describe in detail
- Constraints they mention ("We only have 2 people for 50K invoices/month")

**Emotional signals:**
- Tone changes (frustration, resignation, enthusiasm)
- Values language ("I really care about accuracy")
- Pain admissions ("This is honestly my worst part of the job")

**Social signals:**
- References to how leadership perceives their work ("My CFO would love if we could...")
- Comparisons to peers ("I know [competitor company] automated this")
- Status language ("We want to be seen as a high-tech team")

### Synthesis Question (End of exploration phase):

"If you could only solve one problem for your team, what would it be and why does it matter?"

This single question often surfaces the *true* job underneath all the feature requests.

## Enterprise-Specific Discovery: Buyers vs. Users vs. Gatekeepers

In enterprise, never assume the user equals the buyer. Map this matrix:

```
STAKEHOLDER TYPE      MAIN CONCERN                      INTERVIEW APPROACH
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
User (AP Clerk)       Time savings, accuracy            1:1, open discovery
                      "Does this make my job easier?"   Focus on workflow pain
                      
Buyer (AP Manager)    ROI, team productivity, risk      1:1, problem validation
                      "Does this reduce cost or        Focus on metrics & KPIs
                       improve margins?"

Budget Holder (CFO)   Cost, ROI, implementation risk   1:1, executive briefing
                      "What's the payback period?"     Focus on dollars & timeline
                      
Procurement Officer   Compliance, vendor stability,    1:1 or CAB, requirements
                      integration risk, contract terms  Focus on SLAs, data residency,
                      "Can you meet our standards?"     uptime guarantees
                      
IT Security Lead      Data security, compliance,       1:1, technical deep-dive
                      audit trail, integration         Focus on encryption, SOC2,
                      "Where does our data go?"        access controls
```

### Buyer vs. User Interview Differences

**User Interview Focus:**
- Day-in-the-life workflows
- Pain points in current process
- Emotional friction (frustration, stress)
- Manual workarounds and exceptions
- What would make their job easier
- What they'd use the system for daily

**Buyer Interview Focus:**
- Strategic priorities (cost reduction, compliance, risk mitigation)
- Implementation and integration requirements
- Team structure and change management risk
- Vendor evaluation criteria and constraints
- Contract terms, data residency, SLAs
- Executive sponsor alignment
- Competitive evaluation or replacement timeline

**Sample Buyer Question You'd Never Ask a User:**
"If you had to replace your AP system, what would your procurement process look like? What's the timeline?"

**Sample User Question You'd Never Ask a Buyer:**
"When you see an invoice that doesn't match the PO, what goes through your head? Walk me through the mental process."

### Navigating Procurement Gatekeeping

Procurement and IT security will slow or block your deal if you haven't addressed their concerns early. Discovery questions:

For **Procurement:**
- "Walk me through your vendor evaluation process. From RFP to contract, how many people are involved and how long does it take?"
- "What are your non-negotiables around SLAs, uptime, data residency, and insurance?"
- "What's your experience with vendors that didn't meet their SLAs? How did you handle it?"
- "How do you approach cloud security and data handling in your contracts?"

For **IT Security:**
- "What are your biggest security concerns when evaluating new software-as-a-service products?"
- "Walk me through your data residency and encryption requirements."
- "How often do you do security audits of your vendors? What certifications do you require?"
- "If there was a data breach, how would you want the vendor to inform you? What would your incident response look like?"

## CAB vs. 1:1 Discovery Interviews

**Customer Advisory Board (CAB) Interviews:**
- 6-10 strategic customers in one room (virtually or in-person)
- Annual or bi-annual cadence, 2-3 hours each session
- Structured agenda with mix of presentation, discussion, and feedback
- Strategic-level questions about market trends, competitive positioning, product roadmap priorities
- Not for tactical feature feedback—for validation of big bets

**1:1 Discovery Interviews:**
- Individual users or buyers, 30-45 minutes
- Ongoing cadence (weekly or bi-weekly during active discovery)
- Unstructured exploration of their specific context
- Tactical feature requests, pain points, workflow details
- You learn about their unique situation; they don't influence each other

**When to use each:**
- CAB: "Should we build a mobile app for field teams?" (Strategic bet)
- 1:1: "What fields do you need on the invoice matching screen?" (Feature-level decision)

## Complete Worked Example: Invoice Matching Discovery Interview

**Context:** You're a PM at an enterprise AP automation company. Your hypothesis: enterprise AP teams are spending significant time on invoice-PO mismatch exceptions, and an AI-powered "smart matching" feature could reduce this by 50%+. Your interview subject: Janet, AP Manager at a mid-market consumer goods company (~$50M revenue, 2,500 employees).

### Interview Script (45 minutes)

**OPENING (3 min)**

You: "Janet, thanks so much for taking the time. I'm learning about how accounts payable teams handle invoices that don't match their PO or receipt. There's no right answer to my questions—I just want to understand your reality. This conversation is confidential, and nothing we talk about today will affect your current contract. Sound good?"

Janet: "Of course. I'm curious what you're working on."

You: "Appreciate it. For now, I'm just learning. We can talk about what we're building at the end if there's interest."

**CONTEXT & ROLE (3 min)**

You: "First, walk me through a typical week in your role. What does success look like for you?"

Janet: "I manage a team of four people. We process about 12,000 invoices per month from about 800 vendors. A successful week is when we close the month without carrying exceptions into the next month and our reconciliation is clean."

You: "How long have you been in this role?"

Janet: "Three years. Before that, I was an AP clerk for ten years, so I know the pain from both sides."

You: "That's really valuable context. If I sat here a year from now and you said 'this was a great year,' what would have had to happened?"

Janet: "Honestly? I'd have hired one fewer person and been able to take on the consolidation of our three regional AP centers without adding headcount. Right now, we're drowning."

**CURRENT PROCESS EXPLORATION (10 min)**

You: "Walk me through the entire invoice processing cycle. From the moment an invoice lands—how does it get into your system, and when is it considered 'done'?"

Janet: "Sure. We use SAP. Invoices come in three ways: email, our vendor portal, or EDI for a few large vendors. They drop into our inbox, and our team manually enters them into SAP. We match them against the PO and receipt. SAP's three-way match is supposed to catch discrepancies, but it's crude—it only looks at line-level amounts. If there's any variance, the invoice goes to exception."

You: "Any variance? So even a 1% price difference?"

Janet: "Yeah, and don't even get me started on unit-of-measure issues. We order in cases, but a vendor ships in pallets. SAP gets confused. The invoice sits in exception until someone investigates."

You: "How many invoices hit exception on a typical day?"

Janet: "Out of 500 per day, maybe 100-120 go to exception. That's about 25%."

You: "Walk me through what happens to an exception. How long does it sit? Who touches it?"

Janet: "It shows up on my daily report. I triage. Is it a real discrepancy or a system issue? If it's a system issue—wrong UOM, rounding error, minor price variance—my team either contacts the vendor to clarify or adjusts the invoice in SAP. If it's a real discrepancy, we might hold the invoice until the goods are received or clarified with the vendor."

You: "How long does that take? From exception to resolved?"

Janet: "For 80% of exceptions, we resolve in 1-2 days. The remaining 20% are messy. Wrong quantities, missing receiving documents, price disputes. Those can take 2-3 weeks."

You: "What's one example of a recent exception that was particularly painful?"

Janet: "Two weeks ago, we got an invoice for $85K from a logistics vendor. Their terms are 'net 45 from invoice date.' The PO was for $82K based on an estimate, but their final invoice added a fuel surcharge. SAP rejected it. I had to call them, understand the surcharge calculation, verify it matched our master agreement, update our internal cost forecast, and then manually approve the invoice in SAP. That took me 45 minutes. If I had three more invoices like that that day, I don't have 45 minutes."

You: "That fuel surcharge—does that happen often?"

Janet: "With logistics, yes. With other vendors? Occasionally. And if a vendor ships across multiple invoices, the amounts vary. One vendor we work with ships in three tranches but bills one lump invoice. Nightmare."

You: "How much time per week would you estimate your team spends on exception management?"

Janet: "Our two senior clerks? About 8-10 hours each per week. So 16-20 hours across the team. That's 50-75% of their job, honestly."

**INTERVIEWING OTHER STAKEHOLDERS' NEEDS (5 min)**

You: "You mentioned a team of four. Who else is involved in this process that I should understand?"

Janet: "My CFO cares about cash flow and our payment early-discount capture. She wants to know we're paying on time, not losing money to late fees, but also capturing 2/10 net 30 discounts where we can. Our IT security team just cares that we're not manually entering data into weird systems—audit trail and all that. And frankly, procurement has their own vendor relationships and sometimes pushes back on invoice discrepancies we flag."

You: "Interesting. Has procurement ever told you 'just pay that invoice' even if something was off?"

Janet: "Yes. They'll say, 'That's our largest vendor, we need to maintain the relationship, just fix the invoice internally.' So we end up eating small discrepancies rather than arguing with a key vendor."

**EMOTIONAL & SOCIAL JOB EXPLORATION (5 min)**

You: "When you see an invoice in exception and you know you need to spend 45 minutes investigating, what goes through your head?"

Janet: "Honestly? Resignation. I know I'm burning time that should go to strategic things. And I'm frustrated because the resolution is almost always manual. The exception management is pure busywork."

You: "Strategic things. What would those be?"

Janet: "I'd love to spend more time on vendor scorecards, contract terms optimization, building relationships with our largest vendors. That's the stuff that actually affects our bottom line. But I'm stuck in the weeds."

You: "If I told you that a peer at another company got their exception rate down to 5%, how would that feel?"

Janet: "Honestly? I'd ask how. And I'd feel a bit embarrassed that we're at 25%. My CFO would ask why we're not doing what they're doing."

**PROBLEM VALIDATION (5 min)**

You: "Based on what you've described, it sounds like the core problem isn't matching invoices—it's managing exceptions to the match. The 80% that match automatically are fine. But the 20% exceptions are eating your team's time. Is that fair?"

Janet: "Yes, exactly. If we could just reduce that 20% to 5%, we'd be heroes. That's 50 fewer exceptions per day, times 20 minutes each, times 20 workdays. That's literally 300-400 hours per month we'd get back."

You: "And if you had those 300-400 hours back, what would that mean for your headcount plan?"

Janet: "We're supposed to hire a fifth person in Q2. If we could reduce exceptions by 60%, we could push that hire out to Q3 or Q4. That's one FTE we don't have to pay for."

You: "What would it take for you to believe a solution could actually reduce exceptions that much?"

Janet: "Honestly, I'd want to see it work with our real invoices. Not a demo. A pilot. Our exceptions are often weird, nuanced. Is the AI smart enough to understand fuel surcharges and multi-shipment invoices and UOM mismatches? That's the real test."

**SOLUTION EXPLORATION (5 min)**

You: "We're building something that uses machine learning to understand invoice-to-PO mismatches at a deeper level. Instead of just matching exact amounts, it can understand categories of discrepancies—fuel surcharges, rounding, UOM variances—and either auto-approve them if they're within tolerance, or flag only the real issues that need investigation. The goal is to reduce your exception queue from 25% to 5%, meaning your team spends 10 minutes on real problems instead of 2+ hours on noise."

Janet: "Okay, that's interesting. How would the 'tolerance' get set? Like, how does it know that a 3% fuel surcharge is okay but a 10% variance is not?"

You: "Great question. You'd set the rules. For each vendor, you could say, 'This vendor occasionally adds fuel surcharges—auto-approve anything up to 5% variance.' Or, 'This vendor sometimes combines shipments—auto-approve if total amount is within 2% of PO total.' You maintain control."

Janet: "And what about our SAP integration? Would this sit inside SAP or outside?"

You: "That's something we'd have to figure out. We'd need to pull invoices and POs from SAP, run them through our matching engine, and then either write exceptions back to SAP or update their status. How complex is your SAP configuration?"

Janet: "Pretty standard. We have a consultant who knows it, but honestly, if we have to do a $100K+ integration project, I'll have a hard time justifying it."

You: "That's important to know. We'd need to understand whether we could integrate light-touch, without a big SAP project. Is that a blocker for you?"

Janet: "It would be a major factor, yeah. We'd need it to pay back in under a year. If it's $50K, we need to see $50K+ in labor savings."

**CLOSING (3 min)**

You: "Janet, this is super helpful. I'm hearing that exception management is eating 20% of your team's capacity, and if we could get that down to 5%, it would change your hiring plan and give you time for actual strategic work. And you'd want to see it work with your real invoices before committing to anything. Does that sound right?"

Janet: "Completely. And honestly, if you can do it without a big SAP integration, you'd have something special. Everyone talks about AI, but most of it is vaporware."

You: "I appreciate that reality check. Would you be open to a pilot if we build something that addresses this? No commitment, just test it with your real invoices?"

Janet: "Yeah, I'd be interested. But I'd want to see the integration path first."

You: "Perfect. Can I send you a technical requirements document in a few weeks to get your thoughts? And if we do a pilot, what would you need to see to call it successful?"

Janet: "ROI. Can you reduce my exception rate to under 10% without requiring manual tuning for every vendor? That would be success."

You: "Got it. Can I reach out in three weeks?"

Janet: "Absolutely. I'm excited to see where this goes."

## Synthesis: From Interview to Insight

After conducting 10-12 interviews like Janet's, you'll have hundreds of quotes and observations. Here's how to extract actionable insights:

### Raw Data Organization

Create a simple spreadsheet with columns:
- **Date & Subject** (Janet, AP Manager, ConsumerGoods, Mid-Market)
- **Quote/Observation** ("Exception management is 50% of my team's job")
- **Category** (Pain Point / Metric / Constraint / Emotional / Social / Question)
- **Confidence** (mentioned by 1 person / 3+ people / all enterprise customers)
- **Actionability** (Informs feature / Informs GTM / Informs pricing / Informs ICP)

### Pattern Extraction

Group observations by theme:

**Theme 1: Exception Management is the Bottleneck**
- Janet: "25% of invoices hit exception"
- Marcus (AP Director, Financial Services): "We're at 22%"
- Sarah (AP Manager, Manufacturing): "Maybe 30%, but we just pay them without investigation"
- *Insight*: Across all segments, exception management is 20-30% of volume and consumes 40-50% of team capacity
- *Opportunity*: Feature: Intelligent exception categorization + tolerance-based auto-approval

**Theme 2: Integration Risk is a Deal-Breaker**
- Janet: "If it's a $100K integration project, I won't justify it"
- Marcus: "Our last vendor integration took 6 months"
- IT Security Lead: "We need API documentation and SOC2 certification before we'll allow integration"
- *Insight*: Enterprise customers will reject elegant solutions if implementation is complex or risky
- *Opportunity*: GTM: Publish integration architecture early, get SOC2 cert before public launch, offer managed integration option

**Theme 3: ROI Must Be Clear and Fast**
- Janet: "We need to see ROI in under a year"
- CFO stakeholder input: "If it saves 1 FTE, that's $60K+ value"
- *Insight*: Enterprise buyers don't just want cool—they want measurable payback
- *Opportunity*: Pricing: Tie pricing to labor savings (% of saved FTE cost)

### Insight Quality Rubric

Not all observations are insights. Use this rubric:

**Observation vs. Insight:**
- ❌ Observation: "The user wanted a mobile app"
- ✓ Insight: "AP teams are trying to approve invoices while out of the office during month-end, and mobile access would reduce payment delays by 2-3 days"

**Weak Insight:**
- "Enterprise customers want better reporting"
- *Problem*: Every product manager hears this. Doesn't point to an actionable decision.*

**Strong Insight:**
- "CFOs care about early payment discounts, but AP teams don't have visibility into discount deadlines. This causes $50K+ annual loss per customer. A simple dashboard that surfaces 'invoices eligible for 2/10 discount' would drive ROI and emotional buy-in."
- *Reason*: Tied to a specific metric, a specific stakeholder job, and an actionable feature.*

## Anti-Patterns & How to Avoid Them

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Leading Question

**What it looks like:**
- "Don't you find manual exception handling frustrating?"
- "Wouldn't you love if we could automate this?"
- "SAP is pretty bad at matching, right?"

**Why it's dangerous:**
- Respondent will agree because you've telegraphed the "right" answer
- You hear what you want to hear, not what they actually think
- Confirmation bias gets worse with each leading question

**How to fix it:**
- Use open-ended questions: "Tell me about exception management in your workflow"
- Avoid yes/no questions: "Is this a problem?" → "What challenges do you see?"
- Wait for them to introduce your product area: Don't lead with "problems my solution solves"

### Anti-Pattern 2: Only Interviewing Champions

**What it looks like:**
- The enthusiastic vendor champion who wants your product
- The innovator who's already convinced change is good
- The person who raised their hand to talk to you
- Missing: The skeptics, the risk-averse stakeholders, the people who benefit from status quo

**Why it's dangerous:**
- You build a product that the champion loves but procurement rejects
- You underestimate implementation complexity because you haven't talked to IT
- You miss the real blocker because you only talked to the person with no veto power
- You go to market with messaging that appeals to 10% of buyers and alienates 90%

**How to fix it:**
- Map the stakeholder committee before you interview: Who decides? Who influences? Who might block?
- Specifically ask for skeptics: "I'd love to talk to someone who's considered a vendor like us and decided against it"
- Interview IT, procurement, and finance—not just the user champion
- When you find resistance, lean in: "Tell me more about that concern"

### Anti-Pattern 3: Confusing Feature Requests with Jobs

**What it looks like:**
- User: "I'd love a mobile app"
- You: "Mobile app—got it, adding to the roadmap"
- The job: "I need to approve exceptions while I'm out of the office during month-end"
- The feature: Mobile app

**Why it's dangerous:**
- There might be 5 ways to solve the job; the user suggested one
- The feature might be wrong (email notification beats mobile app)
- You build the feature nobody asked for instead of solving the job
- You ship a nice-to-have instead of a must-have

**How to fix it:**
- When you hear a feature request, ask: "Why would that matter to you?"
- Dig into the underlying job: "What would you do with a mobile app?"
- Test alternative solutions: "Would an email notification when an exception needs approval work just as well?"
- Remember: You're solving jobs, not collecting features

### Anti-Pattern 4: Assuming Your ICP Based on Assumptions

**What it looks like:**
- You assume "large enterprises with 5+ AP people" is your ICP
- You discover that mid-market (100-500 people) feels the pain most acutely because they have fewer people relative to invoice volume
- By the time you realize this, you've built messaging for enterprises and mid-market sales isn't engaging

**Why it's dangerous:**
- Misaligned GTM motion (enterprise sales vs. transactional)
- Wrong pricing model (usage-based vs. per-user vs. ACV)
- Wasted sales effort on accounts that don't have enough pain
- Missing the segment with highest NRR and fastest payback

**How to fix it:**
- Segment your interviews by company size, industry, and invoice volume
- Track where the pain is most acute (not just present)
- Measure willingness to pay by segment
- Let data, not assumptions, define your ICP

## Interview Synthesis Process (Step-by-Step)

1. **Record (with permission) or take detailed notes** on each interview
2. **Transcribe or create a summary** within 24 hours (memory fades)
3. **Tag observations** with theme, stakeholder type, confidence level
4. **Create an affinity map**: Group quotes by theme
5. **Extract 3-5 insights per theme**: What does this pattern tell you about a buying decision or product opportunity?
6. **Validate with second-pass interviews**: Did your early insights hold with later customers?
7. **Prioritize insights by impact**: Which insights, if you acted on them, would have the most effect on your product?
8. **Share findings with cross-functional team**: Engineering, Sales, Marketing need to see the evidence
9. **Build business model hypothesis**: "If we solve this job for this segment, what's the TAM and what's our GTM?"

## Common Pitfalls & Recovery

**Pitfall: Running out of time mid-interview**
- Don't rush the exploration phase to get to your solution
- Use a timer; allocate 60% of time to understanding their world
- It's better to understand deeply with 1 customer than to surface-skim 5

**Pitfall: Customer becomes a feature request backlog**
- You're not a free consultant; you're doing discovery
- If they ask, "Can you add this feature?" → "That's interesting. Why would that matter?"
- Don't say yes; say "I'm learning about this need across several customers, and it's on my list to evaluate"

**Pitfall: Biasing toward "they should buy our product"**
- Stay intellectually honest about misalignment
- If their job doesn't match your product, that's useful data
- Write down: "This customer needs X; we're building Y. Not a fit today."
